Zoning Regulations and Tiny Homes

Zoning laws dictate how land can be used. These laws are determined by local governments like cities and counties and can be quite specific about what types of structures are allowed in different areas. Some common zoning classifications include:

  • Single-family residential
  • Multi-family residential
  • Commercial
  • Industrial
  • Agricultural

Tiny homes are typically classified as either single-family dwellings or recreational vehicles (RVs), and generally:

  • Tiny homes that are built on a permanent foundation are typically treated like traditional site-built homes and must comply with local zoning regulations for single-family homes.
  • Tiny homes on wheels are typically considered RVs and may be allowed in RV parks or on land zoned for recreational use.

Finding Tiny Home-Friendly Locations

The most important step in finding a place to put your tiny home is to research local zoning regulations.

  • Start by contacting the local planning or zoning department. They can provide you with information about the zoning laws in your area and help you determine if there are any restrictions on tiny homes.
  • Attend local planning and zoning board meetings. This is a great way to learn about proposed changes to zoning laws and to voice your support for tiny homes.
  • Join a tiny home advocacy group. These groups can provide you with information and support as you navigate the zoning process.

Once you’ve done your research, you can start looking for land or a parking space for your tiny home.

  • Look for land that is zoned for residential use, or at least recreational use. This will give you the best chance of getting a permit to place your tiny home.
  • Check with local RV parks or campgrounds to see if they allow tiny homes. Some campgrounds have designated areas for tiny home owners.

Legal Challenges to Tiny Homes

In some areas, tiny homes have faced legal challenges from neighbors who are concerned about the impact of these homes on their property values or the character of their neighborhood. Currently, many of these challenges are working their way through the courts.
